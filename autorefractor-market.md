<p><p>The autorefractor market is divided into two primary types: manual and automatic autorefractors. Manual autorefractors require the operator to manually adjust settings and interpret results, offering flexibility and hands-on control, but may be less efficient. In contrast, automatic autorefractors use advanced technology to measure refractive errors with minimal operator intervention, providing quicker and more consistent results. Both types cater to different clinical needs, with manual devices often favored for specialized practices and automatic models preferred in high-throughput settings.</p></p>
